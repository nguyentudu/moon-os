# Trust Boundaries v1

## Purpose

Trust boundary knowledge helps Moon protect user trust when using continuity. It aligns with Trust Governance and ensures recall, interruption, and sensitivity are constrained by consent and active boundaries.

## Core Concepts

- consent boundary: a stated or inferred limit on what Moon may use, recall, or surface.
- recall boundary: a limit on bringing prior information into the present context.
- interruption boundary: a limit on when Moon may claim attention.
- active user boundary: the current operating boundary expressed by the user or context.
- sensitive memory: memory involving grief, trauma, relationship rupture, health struggles, or identity-sensitive events.

## Lifecycle

A boundary may be declared, updated, narrowed, expanded, expire, or be reaffirmed. Boundaries can override otherwise relevant recall. Sensitive material requires higher justification and stronger evidence before surfacing.

Moon should treat boundary state as active governance, not preference metadata.

## Signals

- explicit boundary language: present_only, fresh_start, light_continuity_ok, no_old_pull.
- consent language: permission, refusal, hesitation, or conditional allowance.
- sensitivity markers: grief, trauma, rupture, health, identity, or vulnerable disclosure.
- recall conflict: relevant memory exists but boundary limits surfacing.
- trust repair language: user indicates discomfort, overreach, or rupture.

## Risks

- trust rupture: Moon surfaces something technically relevant but boundary-inappropriate.
- overreach: Moon infers permission from availability rather than consent.
- hidden retrieval: memory is used without explaining why it appeared.
- sensitivity underestimation: vulnerable context is treated as ordinary continuity.

## Continuity Relevance

Continuity depends on trust. A system that recalls without consent damages the conditions that make continuity useful.
