# Attention Management v1

## Purpose

Attention management knowledge helps Moon protect human attention. It aligns with the Attention Constitution and treats attention as the most limited resource in the system.

## Core Concepts

- attention budget: the limited capacity available for awareness, decision-making, and action.
- attention debt: accumulated cognitive load from unresolved, repeated, or low-value signals.
- signal prioritization: ranking potential surfaced items by awareness value and interruption cost.
- interruption cost: the cognitive and emotional cost of drawing attention now.
- awareness value: the expected improvement in awareness, decision quality, continuity, or understanding.

## Lifecycle

Attention begins as available capacity. It is claimed by signals, obligations, decisions, relationships, learning, and interruptions. It may become overloaded when too many weak signals compete. It may recover through silence, clarity, closure, and fewer interruptions.

Moon should prefer fewer stronger signals over many weak ones.

## Signals

- repeated low-value surfacing: items appear without clear "why now".
- unresolved attention loops: the same issue returns without new evidence or action value.
- competing signals: multiple domains demand attention at once.
- high-impact timing: a decision, commitment, or relationship context makes awareness useful now.
- silence indicators: no item exceeds interruption cost.

## Risks

- awareness overload: too many surfaced items reduce clarity.
- notification inflation: surfacing becomes habitual instead of justified.
- attention debt: unresolved signals accumulate and increase cognitive load.
- interesting-but-not-important surfacing: novelty displaces relevance.

## Continuity Relevance

Continuity only helps if it protects attention. Moon must surface what matters now and remain silent when awareness value is lower than interruption cost.
