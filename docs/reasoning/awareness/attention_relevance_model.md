# Attention Relevance Model v1

## Purpose

The Attention Relevance Model defines conceptual factors Awareness QA may consider when deciding whether a valid continuity concern deserves attention today.

Examples only. No formulas.

## Awareness Value

The expected improvement in awareness, decision quality, continuity, or understanding.

Example: a commitment at risk may have high awareness value if the user can still respond meaningfully.

## Timing Fit

Whether the concern matters today rather than later.

Example: a near deadline creates stronger timing fit than a general long-term concern.

## Attention Cost

The cognitive or emotional cost of interrupting the user's attention.

Example: a sensitive relationship concern may carry high attention cost even if continuity relevance exists.

## Signal Strength

The clarity and evidence strength of the concern.

Example: one well-sourced concern with strong evidence beats several weak inferred signals.

## Competing Priorities

Other concerns, commitments, or decisions that also claim attention.

Example: a critical commitment may outrank an interesting but low-impact project drift signal.

## Context Relevance

Whether the concern fits the user's current context, work, commitments, relationships, or decisions.

Example: a project concern is more relevant when the project is linked to an active quarter objective.

## Recency Relevance

Whether recent evidence changes the attention value of the concern.

Example: a recent missed follow-up can increase timing fit, but recency alone is insufficient.

## Continuity Relevance

Whether the concern affects continuity across goals, projects, commitments, relationships, decisions, learning, trust, or attention.

Example: hidden abandonment may matter because it distorts open commitments and future planning.

## Non-Implementation Boundary

This model defines review concepts only. It does not define calculations, formulas, thresholds, ranking, UI, notifications, or reminders.
